Indigenous Research Foundations Course

This project was completed for UPROOT, UBC’s Indigenous pharmacy research team, in partnership with Xwi7xwa Library. It is a 6-module course that covers essential topics for researchers engaging in medical research with Indigenous participants, such as Indigenous data sovereignty, community-based research ethics, working with communities, and systematic searching for Indigenous health topics. Legal Information Quartler for Incarcerated Peoples

The purpose of this project was to create a way for accessible legal information to be delivered to incarcerated people in BC provincial prisons. It resulted in a quarterly publication that balanced the need for in-depth research and quick browsing. It was informed by universal design principles and trauma-informed approaches.

Indigenizing a Science Library Through Green Spaces

The purpose of this assignment was to re-imagine a science library using a social justice lens. Indexing of Medical Literature on Gender Diverse Peoples in Medline and CINAHL Pilot Study

The purpose of this project was to assess the accuracy and completeness of automated indexing in Medline by comparing the indexing of the same articles on gender diverse peoples in CINAHL, which uses manual indexing. In addition to comparing indexing processes, it also assessed how well each controlled vocabulary represented gender diverse groups, such as non-binary, genderfluid, and Two-Spirit peoples.

Revised Short Reflection 1

Identify information needs and respond through the design and provision of information products and services Two projects throughout my program have involved assessing the information needs of a specific group or within a specific context and developing services to address them. While I am proud of these projects and consider them successful, I am closer to confident rather than very confident that I meet this PLO. I believe my past experience in research and tangential skills I have gained in other assignments add to this competency, but I am somewhat worried that actually implementing the proposed projects will be different in the real world. I feel I have the foundations of this competency, but more on the job experience will be necessary to feel entirely confident in performing these tasks independently. The first artifact is a quarterly information resource sent to provincial prisons in British Columbia and was completed by a 4-person group project for LIBR 506. My role was to create the design of the resources, ensuring they align with document accessibility standards, and researching the information needs. The goal was to send this legal information resource regularly enough to build a comprehensive repository of information designed with incarcerated people in mind. It emphasized supporting both quick reference as well as in-depth research while being accessible to all audiences and showing positive, trauma-informed graphic design. While this resource was not designed specifically for Indigenous information seekers, the project acknowledged that Indigenous people are overrepresented in the Canadian prison system and recognized colonialism’s role in this. Research of Indigenous people’s legal information needs was used as the foundation for the content development. I felt I was able to apply the rights of Indigenous Peoples to a very specific situation I was unfamiliar with during this project by relying on publications from Indigenous advocacy organizations to identify information needs, which I can now bring into other work. This project involved thinking about the information needs of a particularly vulnerable and often ignored group that is severely limited by the rules of the Canadian prison system. While the needs were easy to find through extensive research in this area outside of LIS, responding to these needs in a practical way was difficult and required out-of-the-box thinking. In this project, my group was able to compare information resources delivered through many different channels, such as CD-ROM and print materials, and practice accessible design to meet the diverse needs of people within Canadian prisons. My second artifact is a stylized board report that combines an environmental scan of the current state of academic libraries, a strategic plan with goals and objectives, and a budget for the three proposed programs within the strategic plan for a case study of a large university. I was in charge of the economic, cultural, and industry portions of the environmental scan as well as writing the strategic goals and designing the final board report document. The scan found significant concerns around backlash to EDI initiatives and AI threatening the integrity of research and copyright protections, which informed the design of three goals. Two sought to center the commitment to Indigenization and EDI that was in the organization’s mission statement by Indigenizing the Library’s collection and creating a speakers series connecting local BIPOC professionals to students in related disciplines. These proposals were in response to student demographics with at least 10% of the student population being Indigenous and ethical duties, referring to the TRC Calls to Action for information organizations. This project cemented the importance of considering information needs through quantitative data but also through responsibilities to serve your entire community or support a group that could be a part of your community when the proper considerations are made to reduce service barriers.
